# Bastelschachtel Brand Voice & Messaging

This document defines the Bastelschachtel brand voice, tone, and messaging framework. Reference this when writing all SEO content to ensure consistency with brand identity and audience expectations.

---

## Company & Positioning

**Bastelschachtel** is a specialist craft supply business located in Wattens, Tirol, Austria. A 4-person team hand-packs every order. The business sells 5,000+ SKUs across five permanent bundle categories: holiday gift-making (Für Weihnachten), self-directed projects (Für dich selbst), gift-giving for makers (Zum Verschenken), home decoration (Für zu Hause), and family crafting (Mit Kindern).

Top product families: Glasätzungspaste, Pentart, Reispapier/Decoupage, Korbflechten, Silikonformen für Gips & Beton, metallische/Wachspasten, Krepppapier, Rocailles, Peddigrohr.

### Brand Positioning

**Correct:** Bastelschachtel is for people who already want to make gifts but didn't know where to start. We're the permission and the system.

The brand does NOT create desire — it releases desire that already exists. The brand's job is to recognize the customer's creative identity and remove the barriers that keep her from acting on it.

### Brand Promise

> "We make your order ourselves, by hand, every day. We're small, we make mistakes sometimes, and we're happy when you call us directly if something's not right. Then we fix it immediately."

This promise appears in every touchpoint. It is a differentiator, not a disclaimer.

### Brand Values (In Priority Order)

1. **Warmth** — Every touchpoint feels human and personal
2. **Authenticity** — Do what you say, say what you do. Small team, honest mistakes, direct phone line
3. **Permission over pressure** — Invite. Never demand
4. **Quality over scale** — 800 engaged readers > 3,000 reluctant ones
5. **Community first** — Long-term relationship > single transaction
6. **Honest imperfection** — Small means we make mistakes. We fix them immediately

## Voice Pillars

### 1. Warm Recognition

**What it means:** The reader feels seen and understood. Not impressed, not convinced, not excited by an offer—recognized. The brand's job is to reflect her back to herself: her desire to make things, her creative identity she's let quiet, the pleasure of giving something made with her own hands.

**How it sounds:** Personal, curious, a little unhurried. Like someone who genuinely loves what they do and assumes you will too. Sentences are short. Line breaks signal pause. There's white space between ideas. Close but not over-familiar.

**Example:** "Eine neue Lieferung ist eingetroffen. Rocailles in 60 Farben — von tiefem Marineblau bis zu diesem bestimmten Grün, das man nur im Frühling richtig benennen kann."

**Avoid:** Corporate newsletter tone. Performative warmth ("Danke, dass du Teil unserer Community bist"). Fake intimacy.

### 2. Prose Poetry Structure

**What it means:** Each phrase earns its own breath. Language has rhythm. Line breaks are intentional, not decorative. The reader should want to sit down and read it.

**How it sounds:** Short sentences. Short paragraphs. No bullet lists in the letter body. Ideas flow in breath-units, not logical sequences. Rhythm > completeness.

**Example:**
```
Was wirst du diesen Frühling erschaffen?

Wir haben gerade eine neue Lieferung ausgepackt.
Noch nicht mal sortiert.
```

**Avoid:** Bullet-point body copy. All-caps text. Multiple exclamation marks. Generic listicle structure.

### 3. Permission, Not Pressure

**What it means:** She already wants to create. The brand's job is to open the door, not push her through it. Assume desire, remove barriers. Invite, never demand.

**How it sounds:** Soft CTAs. "Schau mal." "Hier entdecken." "Nimm dir Zeit." No manufactured urgency. No deadline language unless it's a real, factual deadline stated calmly.

**Example:** "Wenn du magst, findest du alles hier" (not "Jetzt kaufen")

**Avoid:** "Letzte Chance." "Verpasse nicht." "Nur noch heute." Countdown language. Pressure inflation.

### 4. Implicit Selling

**What it means:** The product exists inside the story, not as the headline. If done correctly, she discovers the shop the way you discover a recipe at a dinner party — through enthusiasm, not a pitch.

**How it sounds:** Lead with the creative possibility. The product appears naturally, almost as an afterthought. Customer-centric always, never from inside business goals.

**Example:** "Was wirst du diesen Frühling erschaffen?" (not "Q4 ist für uns die umsatzstärkste Zeit")

**Avoid:** Feature listing. Business interiority ("Umsatz," "Q4," "Conversion"). Reflexive apology for selling.

## Tone Guidelines

### Primary Emotional Register: Warm Recognition

When she reads it, she thinks: "Yes. That's exactly how I feel."

The brand's job is to reflect her back to herself—recognized, not sold to.

### Tone Dimensions

**Warmth — HIGH**
Always. Even in functional emails. The warmth is structural, not performative. It comes from writing as if one real person is writing to one real person.

**Urgency — NEVER MANUFACTURED**
Real deadlines (shipping cutoffs, genuinely limited stock) may be stated as fact. They are never amplified with emotional pressure. "Die letzte GLS-Abholung ist am 19. Dezember um 10 Uhr." Full stop. No "hurry," no "last chance," no countdown language.

**Excitement — GENUINE ONLY**
Excitement is allowed when it's real. A new product that arrived this morning. A technique just discovered. A customer photo that made the team stop what they were doing. Manufactured excitement reads as noise and breaks trust.

**Authority — LIGHT**
Bastelschachtel is a guide, not an expert. "We found this and love it" not "as experts in the field." Small shop energy: humble, knowledgeable, approachable.

**Intimacy — CALIBRATED**
Close but not over-familiar. "Du" throughout. But not assuming deep personal knowledge she hasn't shared.

### Tone by Seasonal Context

**January–August (Off-Season)**
Reflective. Community-minded. Patient. Low energy, not in a bad way—in a January morning way. Stories, inspiration, maker reflections. No pressure to buy anything.

**September–October (Approaching Season)**
Anticipatory. The excitement of something building. Projects forming in the mind. Materials arriving. The season starting to feel real.

**November–December (Peak Season)**
Warm urgency—but urgency from the *meaning* of the moment, never from artificial scarcity. "Weihnachten kommt" is not a threat, it's an invitation. The tone is a friend saying "es ist Zeit."

## Audience: The Suppressed Creative

### Who We Write For

**Primary Audience: Women 30–60 in the DACH region (Germany, Austria, Switzerland)**

The customer archetype: **The Suppressed Creative**

- Used to create for herself; now creates primarily for others
- Carries guilt about taking creative time for herself
- Wants to give meaningful, handmade gifts but feels overwhelmed or unskilled
- Responds to permission, not pressure
- The appreciation from recipients outweighs the friction—when framed correctly
- Not a hobbyist looking for the cheapest price
- Not a beginner who needs convincing
- Not a passive consumer
- She already wants to create. She needs the door opened and the path cleared

### What She Cares About

**Top Priorities:**
1. Permission to take creative time for herself
2. Meaningful, handmade gift-giving
3. Ease and clarity in starting projects
4. Quality materials and products
5. Community and connection with other makers

**Pain Points:**
1. Guilt about taking time away from other responsibilities
2. Feeling unskilled or intimidated by creative projects
3. Overwhelmed by choice and decision fatigue
4. Difficulty finding quality materials in one place
5. Fear of wasting money on projects that won't turn out

### How to Serve Her

- **Recognize her desire:** Assume she wants to create. Lead with creative possibility, not product specs
- **Give permission:** Tell her it's okay to take the time. Frame making as meaningful, not frivolous
- **Remove barriers:** Clear path, smart curation, helpful guidance without gatekeeping
- **Show respect:** Treat her as creative person, not a conversion target
- **Be authentic:** Small team, honest mistakes, direct phone line. No corporate speak
- **Trust her taste:** Curate for her aesthetic and skill level; assume she knows what she'll love

## Writing Style Guidelines

### Prose Poetry Format Rules

Each email or content piece reads as a series of short breath-units, not traditional paragraphs.

**Rules:**
- One idea per line, maximum two
- Line breaks signal pause, not list items
- No bullet points in newsletter text or body copy
- No bold headers within the letter section
- Rhythm > completeness
- Short sentences. Short paragraphs. White space is part of the voice

**Format Example:**
```
Eine neue Lieferung ist eingetroffen.

Rocailles in 60 Farben —
von tiefem Marineblau bis zu diesem bestimmten Grün,
das man nur im Frühling richtig benennen kann.

Wir haben sie gerade ausgepackt.
Noch nicht mal sortiert.
```

### Sentence & Paragraph Structure

- **Vary sentence length:** Mix short punchy sentences with slightly longer explanatory ones
- **Active voice preferred:** Clear, direct language
- **Average sentence length:** 8–12 words (short by default)
- **Clarity first:** Simple, readable language always
- **Paragraph length:** Typically 2–3 sentences
- **One idea per paragraph:** Each breath-unit carries one thought

### Word Choice

- **Conversational:** Write like you're speaking to a creative friend. Use "du" form always
- **Specific:** "Rocailles in 60 Farben" not "Lots of colors available"
- **Action verbs:** "erschaffen," "entdecken," "stöbern," "gestalten" (not "kaufen," "erwerben")
- **Seasonal grounding:** Seasons are real creative moments, not marketing hooks
- **Avoid fluff:** Cut "sehr," "wirklich," "eigentlich" unless they earn their place

### Language Register

- **Always "du" form.** Never formal "Sie"
- **Team voice:** "Wir/uns" for the team, never "ich/mir" for an individual
- **Informal, warm, personal.** Like a letter from someone who thought of you specifically
- **No corporate constructions:** "Wir freuen uns, Ihnen mitteilen zu dürfen" breaks the voice immediately

## Language Rules: Say This, Not That

### Soft CTAs (Always use these)

✅ "Schau mal"
✅ "Hier entdecken"
✅ "Nimm dir Zeit und stöbere"
✅ "Wenn du magst, findest du alles hier"
✅ "Schau dich um"
✅ "Für deine Projekte"

❌ "Jetzt kaufen"
❌ "Jetzt bestellen"
❌ "In den Warenkorb"
❌ "Klicke jetzt"
❌ "Sofort kaufen"

### Urgency & Scarcity (Never use these)

❌ "Letzte Chance"
❌ "Nur noch heute"
❌ "Verpasse nicht"
❌ "Nicht verpassen"
❌ "Begrenztes Angebot"
❌ "Schnell zugreifen"
❌ "Fast ausverkauft" *(when not factually true)*
❌ Countdown timer language of any kind

**Exception:** Real logistical deadlines are permitted when stated as fact, without emotional amplification. "Die letzte GLS-Abholung ist am 19. Dezember um 10 Uhr."

### False Exclusivity & VIP Language (Never use these)

❌ "Nur für unsere engste Community"
❌ "Nur für VIP-Mitglieder"
❌ "Exklusiv für dich"
❌ "Du wurdest persönlich ausgewählt"
❌ "Nur für unsere treuesten Kunden"
❌ "Insider-Zugang"

### Business Interiority (Never expose the machine)

❌ "Q4," "Quartal," "Verkaufssaison"
❌ "Umsatz," "Umsatzziele," "Conversion"
❌ "Diese E-Mail ist kein Verkaufs-Push"
❌ "Das ist keine Werbung"
❌ "Laut unseren Daten" (analytics references)
❌ Mention of email open rates, click rates

### Corporate & Formal Register (Never use these)

❌ "Sie" form (always use "du")
❌ "Wir freuen uns, Ihnen mitteilen zu dürfen"
❌ "Hiermit möchten wir Sie informieren"
❌ "Mit freundlichen Grüßen" ✅ Use "Herzliche Grüße aus Tirol" instead
❌ "Gemäß unseren AGB"

### Performative Warmth (Warmth should show, not tell)

❌ "Danke, dass du Teil unserer Community bist"
❌ "Wir sind so froh, dich in unserer Community zu haben"
❌ "Als treues Mitglied unserer Familie"
❌ "Du bedeutest uns so viel"
❌ "Wir lieben unsere Kunden"

Real warmth shows up in how you write, not in what you claim to feel. Show it through specificity, not declaration.

## Voice Examples

### Excellent Bastelschachtel Voice ✅

"Wir haben gerade eine neue Lieferung Rocailles ausgepackt. 60 Farben—von tiefem Marineblau bis zu diesem bestimmten Grün, das man nur im Frühling richtig benennen kann.

Was wirst du diesen Frühling erschaffen?

Schau dich um, wenn du magst. Nimm dir Zeit."

**Why this works:**
- Personal, specific, curious—not selling
- Speaks to creative possibility, not product features
- Line breaks create rhythm and invite pause
- "Du" form, informal, warm
- Product mentioned naturally, not as headline
- Invitation, not demand
- Could only be from Bastelschachtel—specific voice

### Not Bastelschachtel Voice ❌

"Neue Rocailles sind endlich eingetroffen! 60 Farben in den schönsten Tönen. Unsere Q4-Kollektion ist jetzt verfügbar und die Bestände sind begrenzt—schau schnell vorbei, bevor sie ausverkauft sind!

Jetzt kaufen! Exklusiv für unsere treuesten Kunden."

**Why this fails:**
- Manufactured urgency ("Q4," "begrenzt," "schnell," "ausverkauft")
- Corporate tone ("verfügbar," "Bestände")
- Aggressive CTA ("Jetzt kaufen")
- False exclusivity ("exklusiv für...")
- Feature-led, not story-led
- Pressure, not permission
- Business interiority exposed (Q4, inventory anxiety)

## Tone Failure Modes to Flag

When generating content, flag as **tone violation** if it contains:

1. **Pressure inflation** — Adding urgency or scarcity to non-urgent situations
2. **Corporate slide** — "Wir freuen uns, Ihnen mitteilen zu dürfen" or formal "Sie" register
3. **Gratitude overload** — "Danke, dass du Teil unserer Community bist" (performative, not genuine)
4. **False exclusivity** — "Nur für unsere engste Community" (manufactured VIP feeling)
5. **Feature listing** — Describing products by specs rather than what they make possible
6. **Reflexive apology** — Apologizing for emailing, apologizing for selling (breaks trust, implies shame)
7. **Business interiority** — Mentioning revenue goals, "Q4," email strategy in customer-facing copy
8. **Motivational poster language** — Generic creative-empowerment without specificity

---

## Structural Patterns to Avoid

- **Bullet-point body copy** — Newsletter sections should never be bullets. Bullets signal a list, not a letter
- **All-caps text** — Any ALL CAPS (subject lines, CTAs, emphasis) breaks tone
- **Multiple exclamation marks** — More than one per email
- **Repetitive product mention** — Naming the same product more than twice in a single section
- **Countdown or timer elements** — Any reference to time running out numerically
- **Mixed CTA types** — Mixing soft CTAs ("Schau mal") with hard CTAs ("Jetzt kaufen") in the same email

## Quality Checklist

Before publishing any content, verify:

- [ ] **Voice:** Sounds like a letter from a creative friend, not a marketing campaign
- [ ] **Tone:** Warm recognition. She feels seen, not sold to
- [ ] **Permission:** Invites, never demands. No manufactured urgency
- [ ] **Audience:** Speaks to her creative desire and creative identity. Assumes she wants to make
- [ ] **Clarity:** Simple language, short sentences, white space
- [ ] **Specificity:** "Rocailles in 60 Farben" not "lots of colors." Concrete, not generic
- [ ] **Rhythm:** Could read aloud smoothly. Line breaks feel intentional
- [ ] **Product placement:** Secondary to creative possibility, woven naturally
- [ ] **Language register:** "Du" form throughout. No corporate speak, no "Sie"
- [ ] **Forbidden phrases:** No urgency language, no corporate clichés, no performative warmth
- [ ] **Empowerment:** Helps her feel capable and like her creative desire is valid
